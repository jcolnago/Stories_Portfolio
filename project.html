A significant part of how we think about issues in our daily lives is framed by how these issues are communicated in the media. We have seen this happen with political issues, environmental issues, and more recently we have seen how the narrative surrounding technology influences people's behaviors.

In this project my data analysis objective is to map how the issue of online data privacy has been treated by different news outlets and, with the skills acquired in class, I hope to engage the reader in a critical discussion on how their own opinions and behaviors might have been influenced by the content that they have consumed so far. My ultimate goal, or "call to action" for the reader, is to revise their data privacy news diet to include a more diverse set of sources.

I find it important to highlight that at this time I do not know what the news coverage surrounding privacy looks like beyond gut feelings and personal experience. This story and goals might need to be significantly revised depending on what I find.

The Data Source

The GDELT Project

November 17th, 2019

For the final project I chose to rely on the data set provided by the GDELT Project in particular the 2.0 version of their Global Knowledge Graph. As described on their website:

... the GDELT 2.0 Global Knowledge Graph extracts each person name, organization, company, disambiguated location, millions of themes and thousands of emotions from each article, resulting in an annotated metadata graph over the world's news each day. Totaling over 200 million records and growing at a rate of half a million to a million articles a day, the GKG 2.0 is perhaps the worldâ€™s largest open data graph over global human society ...

My plan is to query this dataset for a subset of news outlet relevant to the U.S. based public, focusing my queries with the "online privacy" keyword. Leveraging their emotional undercurrent assessment for each article I plan to map out not only how news coverage about online privacy has evolved over time, but also how the sentiment associated with those articles have evolved over time. I further plan to combine categorizations of news sources based on their status (e.g. newspaper vs blog) and their political leanings (e.g. right vs left). For the first I'll rely on personal knowledge of the outlets selected and, for the latter I plan to rely on the Media Bias/Fact Check website to help me subset the news outlet in different categories.

The Story Telling Tool

Shorthand

November 17th, 2019

At first I toyed with the idea of creating a more interactive "chose your own adventure" style of story telling which would rely more heavily on web development skills. However, I quickly realized that this meant creating multiple narratives, which might be overly complicated for the timespan that I have in this class.

Nevertheless I really enjoy articles that tell me the story they are trying to convey with a set of more interactive graphs to help visualize what is happening. Because of this I was very happy to be introduced to Shorthand as it seems to simplify the infrastructure creation process to achieve this format.

Time permitting, I would love to create a visualization that lets readers try a combination of different news sources to try to create a "balanced diet guide" for themselves.

For this part I drafted a storyboard for my project using Shorthand, and the version before feedback can be found here.

Next I designed my user research protocol focusing on millenials and generation X users as they are the population most likely to be technically savvy enough to care about this topic, while also being in-tune enough with news outlets to be more significantly influenced by what I describe. To find these people I leveraged my social and family network, as I have several individuals that fit this profile.
My interview script was very simple and tried to accomplish the following:

  • Identify if the topic was of interest to them;
  • Identify if the place holder visualizations would be enough to convey the message I was trying to convey; and,
  • Check if this sort of article would make them willing to engage with the call to action at the end.
