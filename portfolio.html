I have been trying to learn how to communicate data and the story I want to tell with that data more effectively. I feel that during the PhD we learn how to do it efficiently, but the visualizations and data representations tend to not be very interesting or captivating.

This page documents my progress in communiciating stories with data during the class Telling Stories with Data (94870-B2) taught by Chris Goranson in the Fall/2019.


Leaky Data

Assignment 3 & 4

November 7th, 2019

For this assignment I chose to redesign the graph from Wired's "The Evolution of Data Leaks" for a few different reasons:

  • The graphs show in the original data visualization did not have the same scale, potentially causing readers to come to incorrect conclusions;
  • The website opens with a Horizon graph which I find particularly difficult to parse; and,
  • Even though the audience of Wired would probably find the existing information in the graph interesting and sufficient, I believe this is important data that should be reacheable to a wider audience.

Given the above described reasons I chose to focus on picking a chart format that would allow for comparison between the different categories in the same scale, and providing key information to non-tech savvy readers to understand the main takeaways from the graph.
I originally played with the idea of a line graph, but after quickly plotting the graph on Excel I realized that the lines would be overlapping too much and hard to parse.


Then I decided to try a stacked area chart. After deciding that this addressed my concerns I moved to finding a way to keep the aspects that I liked about the graph (e.g. how it is interactive). The way I found to do that was to use the free infographic maker called Venngage. This allowed me to quickly prototype an interactive stacked area chart and test it with my lab mates.


I got a number of different feedback. As positive feedback, they were able to correctly assess what the graph was showing, even without the added information the original graph had. This did not surprise me since they are both tech and security savvy, but one of them said that they could see this being used in a presentation for people who did not understand the technical aspects of it. They also liked the colors as a whole and the fact that the main difference was using the strongest color. Their improvements focused on a few different aspects. First they both pointed out that the legend labels were not uniformly capitalized and not all in one line. One of them was very adamant about needing to caption the x-axis with "years," even though he conceded it was obvious that those were years. They both also wanted more information about the scope and a precise definition of the terms ("I'm not sure what skimming means and hacking is a very broad term"). One of them said that a description of what the graph is describing could be helpful for other people to process it. Lastly, neither realized that they could interact with the legends.
Based on their suggestions, I revised the graph to its final form:

For the final graph I added a few takeaways that were part of the original graph, I fixed the legend labels capitalization and move it to be vertical, I added the x-axis label in a smaller font, and I added a note to indicate that the reader can interact with the legend as well. My rationale for moving the legend to be vertical was that it both ensured they would be in one row, but also it meant that the labels would match the order shown in the graph, creating another level of association to help the reader parse the graph.
Note: The color pallete was based on Wes Anderson's movies and I used Coolors.co to reproduce it.

OECD data visualization

Assignment 1

November 3rd, 2019

In this assigment we used the OECD general government debt data set, which describes the total debt-to-GDP ratio as a percentage of the total GDP for each OECD country.

G7 countries top the list of worse government debt

For this first graph I used the built-in graph generator in the OECD website, highlighting the G7 countries. Unfortunately that generator does not gives us control over color, so we end up with a rainbow color scheme.


Government debt as a percentage of GDP over the years

For this first graph I used the beeswarm template from RAWGraphs. As per the instruction assignments I modified the width and height, the x-axis legend, as well as the color and maximum size for the circles. One thing I appreciate about this graph is the ability to see how the ratios changed over time for each country, which we did not see in the previous graph (bar chart). I found the graph very aesthetically pleasing but, as is, it is hard to derive information from it as my eyes just glaze over all of the circles.


The global economic crisis wasn't bad for everyone

The 2008 global economic crisis is considered to have been the most serious financial crisis since the Great Depression of the 1930s. However, not all countries seemed to have suffered the same for it. In the graph below we see that Sweden, Switzerland, Norway, and Israel seem to have started, or continued, a downward trajectory in the debt-to-GDP ratio at the onset of the crisis. This means that, at a time that the vast majority of OECD countries were dealing with worsening debt-to-GDP ratios, these countries managed to continue to reduce their debt, increase their GDP, or both.
For this graph I chose to use an area graph as the bump graph felt misleading at times (for example, due to the lack of data for Japan for 2018, the bump graph makes it seem like the ratio dropped very significantly). This type of graph also allows us to easily identify areas of change in the ratio, displaying a "waist" or "cone" shape depending on the trend. This can also be visualized in the beeswarm graph, but it is harder to notice a clear difference. I chose to use an acqua green, as opposed to a simple green, to indicate that the highlighted countries indicate a more positive trend over the other countries, even though they may not have the best ratios in absolute terms. Lastly, I chose to make the graph narrower to help highlight the patterns in the data within the bi-annual timeframe, and made the 2008 graph marker a darker color to facilitate navigating the graph, since it is long.
